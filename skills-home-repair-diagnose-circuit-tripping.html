DIAGNOSE WHY A CIRCUIT KEEPS TRIPPING

Steps:
1. Turn the tripped breaker OFF. Unplug everything on that circuit.
2. Reset the breaker to ON.
3. If it trips immediately with nothing plugged in: the problem is in the wiring. Call an electrician.
4. If it holds: plug devices back in one at a time, waiting 30 seconds between each.
5. If it trips when a specific device is plugged in: that device has a fault. Stop using it.
6. If it trips only when multiple devices run simultaneously: the circuit is overloaded. Move high-draw devices (space heaters, hair dryers, microwaves) to different circuits.
7. If it trips randomly under normal load: the breaker itself may be worn. Replace the breaker (or call an electrician).

Overload Math:
- Most household circuits are 15A (1800W) or 20A (2400W).
- Add up wattage of everything on the circuit. If it exceeds 80% of capacity (1440W or 1920W), redistribute.

Notes:
- A breaker that trips immediately with nothing connected = possible short circuit or ground fault in the wiring. Do not reset repeatedly. Call an electrician.
- A warm or discolored breaker indicates internal failure. Call an electrician.
- AFCI breakers trip on arc faults (loose connections). Check all outlets and switches on the circuit for loose wires.
- If you smell burning or see scorch marks anywhere, cut the main breaker and call an electrician immediately.
